The Holt-Winter Forecasting Procedure on Walmart Selling Dataset

Abstract

The Holt-Winters method is a time-series forecasting technique that combines elements of trend decomposition, exponential smoothing, and moving averages. It is used to predict the future behavior of a time series based on historical data, allowing users to make accurate and reliable forecasts across a wide range of applications, including finance, stock management, and production planning. This paper presents a Holt-Winters method applied to Walmart weekly sales forecast, as well as a comparison between the Additional and Multiplicative Seasonal Models in which the multiplicative performed better. A breathily exploratory analysis is performed adding external features such as average temperature and fuel price, showing that some departments have a correlation with those exogenous data.

keywords: HOLT-WINTERS METHOD; TIME-SERIES; FORECASTING; EXPONENTIAL SMOOTHING; PYTHON; NUMPY;

1. Introduction

Department stores present areas, called departments, specialized in a product category. In each category, the sales rate behaves in a particular way according to its own seasonality and tend. The Holt-Winter procedure is a simple exponential smoothing method widely used for forecasting, which can cope with level, trend, and seasonality. Therefore, this method is applied to Walmart Selling Dataset aiming to predict the next nine weeks’ sales.

To this end, a Holt-Winter model is built from scratch. Along the way, some practical problems are faced e discussed, including methods to find stating values, choice of smoothing parameters, and the choice between multiplicative and additive seasonal models. Also, a comparison between the multiplicative seasonal model and additive seasonal models is performed.

2. Methodology CRISP-DM

SHEARER, Colin. 2002

The Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining was first conceived in 1996. It was intended to be an industry-, tool, and application-neutral model.

CRISP-DM organizes the data mining process into six phases followed by this project: business understanding, data understanding, data preparation, modeling, evaluation, and deployment.

Phase one, Business Understanding, is considered the most important. It focuses on understanding the project objectives from the business perspective, converting this knowledge into a data mining problem definition.

The Data Understanding phase starts with initial data collection and involves three more steps, including the description of data, exploration of data, and the verification of data quality.

(SHEARER, 2000, p. 5)

The Data Preparation phase covers all activities to construct the final data set that will be fed into the modeling tool from the initial raw data. Tasks include table, record, and attribute selection, as well as transformation and cleaning of data.

The fourth phase is Modeling. This step includes the selection of the modeling technique, the generation of the test design, the creation of the model, and the assessment of models.

Before proceeding to the final deployment of the model built, the results must be evaluated based on business success criteria, including a final statement about whether the project already meets the initial business objectives.

The Deployment phase can be as simple as generating a report or as complex as implementing a repeatable data mining process.

3. Business Understanding

One of the leading retail stores in the US, Walmart, would like to predict sales accurately. A sales forecast helps businesses estimate potential future sales. It also allows businesses to anticipate their future needs such as workforce, resources, cash flow, inventory, and investment capital.

There are certain events and holidays which impact sales on each day. Walmart runs several promotional markdown events preceding these holidays: the Super Bowl, Labour Day, Thanksgiving, and Christmas.

In this project, the objective is to forecast the total sales for the next nine weeks. Assuming that the unique information available is the sales time series from the last three years.

Additionally, the dataset presents the average temperature in the region, the cost of fuel over time, and the unemployment rate.

6. HoltWinter Model

6.1 Data Split

After the preparation phase, the data ends up with 143 rows. That represents the number of weeks available to perform the model train and test.

The Holt-Winter procedure demands the following data segmentations:

  • At least two seasons to calculate the centered moving mean. The interval between weeks 0 and 103.

In the centered moving mean calculation, half of the first season, and half of the last one are lost. The first interval is represented with the red area, the last half will be reutilized to train the model.

  • The initial seasonal index is defined for the 52 weeks of the year.
  • The remaining data, the weeks 79 until 143 are used at the expanding window train and validation. Note that at the gap [90:104] the shadows from the moving mean calculation and from the expanding window overlap, indicating that the data belongs to both phases.

6.2. Initial values of model parameters

6.2.1. Seasonal Indice

The initial seasonal indices can be computed by comparing the appropriate observation in the first year with X1ˉ\bar{X_1}X1​ˉ​﻿. In the aditive seasonal model, the equation used is F0=(xm−X1ˉ)F_0 = (x_m - \bar{X_1} )F0​=(xm​−X1​ˉ​)﻿, and F0=xm/X1ˉ F_0 = x_m / \bar{X_1}F0​=xm​/X1​ˉ​﻿ in the multiplicative seasonal model (Granger & Newbold,1986, Chapter 5). Instead of using a unique mean value of X for the first year, a centered moving mean is calculated to replace that reference value.

Centered Moving Mean

The CMM is calculated using the mean of the mmm﻿ surrounding values at the position ttt﻿. Where mmm﻿ is the season length and ttt﻿ is the position that is calculated. As long as a year has 52 weeks, in order to estimate 52 means, it is necessary to use 104 weeks peace of information from the time series.

Winters (1960) originally recommended that the seasonal factor should be normalized at the start of the series but not thereafter so that they would soon “contain some of the trend effects”. Roberts (1982) states that later authors suggested that they should be normalized once a year or even at every observation in order to be "consistent with any reasonable definition of seasonality” ( 1988, p.133, apud Chatfield & Mohammad).

In this project, the normalization is performed only at the beginning. Considering that the training data and the prediction horizon are short, the constant normalization would not make a relevant impact on the results.
